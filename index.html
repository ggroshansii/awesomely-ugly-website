Characters:

Jerry

Jerry is the character that glues the show together. He is the observant to all of the ridiculous things that happen between the other main characters, often using his trademark observational jokes in the process. He does not seem to have much emotion throughout the show Elaine points out that he has "never felt remorse," to which Jerry replies, "Yeah, I feel kinda bad about that." Jerry, George and Elaine all share a general trait of not letting go of other character's remarks and going to great lengths to be proven right. In one episode, Jerry goes out of his way to rent a house in Tuscany, Italy, just because "The Maestro" told him there weren't any available. He is maybe the least exciting character in a way, but an interesting aspect he brings is his relationships. He almost has a new relationship every episode always finding a small flaw that causes him to break up with each woman.

George

George is neurotic, self-loathing, and not intelligent. He is constantly scheming to try to get out of work whether it is reading a book or having people bother him at his office. He has went to a strangers house to watch Breakfast at Tiffany's, (a 90 page book) he has acted frustrated anytime anyone saw him at work to make people think he was really busy, and he has even slept under his desk for extended periods of time. Though George is displayed as a generally unintelligent man, in one episode he is forced to be abstinent because his girlfriend has mono. This leads him to access a whole new part of his brain that is no longer focused on sex. During this time he learns a new language in a day, coaches Yankee players on their swing, and many other feats that ordinary George would not be capable of. He brings a certain charm to the show that no other character brings, maybe it is his constant agony that brings all of us the joy we get from watching George.

Elaine

Elaine is intelligent and assertive. She claims that she has gotten a 140 on an IQ test and takes one for George because he wanted his girlfriend to think that he was smart, unfortunately she got very stressed and angry resulting in a 90 on the test. Elaine has also thrown Georges toupee out Jerry’s window yelling about how she hates it. Another trait of Elaine’s is her superficiality, cited by this incident, After a rock climbing accident mangles Tony's face, Elaine admits to Jerry that she can't date someone who isn't attractive and wonders how long she is obligated to stay with him post-accident. She is the only real main female character showing up in almost every episode. She adds a necessary feminine side to the group.

Kramer

Kramer is a one of a kind character he is eccentric, goofy, a wannabe entrepreneur, and many other things. A few examples of his entrepreneurial pursuits are the pizza place where you make your own pie, the bro, (a bra for a guy) and a cologne that smells like the ocean which the idea is actually stolen by Calvin Klein much to the dismay of Kramer. Another characteristic that Kramer is known for is his odd body movements and sudden outbursts such as saying “Yo Yo Ma” randomly. He is known to mooch off of his friends constantly, mostly from Jerry’s kitchen. Kramer brings an aspect of unpredictability and also gets his friends into trouble by convincing them of doing illegal things such as urinating in a parking garage and parking in a handicap spot. Combine all of these things and you have a character that can be labeled a “fan favorite.”

Main Settings:

Jerry's Apartment

Whether Jerry is on the phone, his friends are visiting him, or he and Kramer are chatting about something hilarious, it's hard to think of a Seinfeld episode that doesn't include this set. Viewers learned so much about this character from the way that he has set up his apartment, as his love of cereal is on display in the kitchen, and he has very simple furniture. This is the place where Jerry becomes closer with Kramer, invites dates over, and wonders if he and Elaine should be more than friends again.

Monk's Cafe

The gang always meets her to grab a meal, have some coffee, and chat about what's going on in their lives. They have some hilarious and seemingly meaningless conversations here, and it's a good place to observe their friendship dynamic and the fact that Seinfeld is a "show about nothing." If Jerry isn't home, there's a good chance that he's here.

Kramer's Apartment

Kramer's apartment is as quirky as he is with pasta figures near the couch and many strange objects that the other characters wouldn't have in their homes. This is the third most significant location on Seinfeld. Since Kramer lives so close to Jerry, he's often part of the action, and viewers get to see his goofy world regularly. Chances are that even when Jerry is having a boring day at home, Kramer is up to something interesting.

George's Parents Apartment

George's parents stress him out and it's tough when he has to move back home after he doesn't have many prospects in life. Estelle and Frank's house is another important set on this show, as it's where George spends much of his time. Viewers are familiar with seeing the family fighting here, as they don't seem to have conversations that don't involve screaming and tension, and it's where George examines the state of his life, wishing that things were going much better for him.

The Comedy Club

The comedy club where Jerry does stand-up sets up many episodes and gives some insight into what Jerry is thinking.

NYC Streets & Subways

While Seinfeld's NYC depiction isn't perfect, the show does offer a glimpse of the daily life of a few New Yorkers. The streets of New York City are definitely a major setting on the sitcom, as the characters are often walking around and finding themselves in hilarious, silly situations. They often run into people who they know, which can be awkward since they don't love everyone. The characters also get stuck on the subway in one memorable episode, making this a major setting, too. If not for the other important locations, this would be ranked much higher.
